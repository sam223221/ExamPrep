Software Architecture and Agile Development (5/5)
▶The systematic literature review of Yang et al. (2016) provide
many further tips to improve the alignment between
architecture and agile development – among these are:
1. Stakeholders – including through their non-functional
requirements from which quality controls are derived – should
engage in decisions about any freezes made to an architecture
2. Also track architectural issues in a backlog
3. Keep architectural documentation up to date, and also
document iteratively and incrementally as you go
4. Use tools (from photographed blackboard design sketches to
ticketing systems and at least occasionally formal tools)
▶While they list many more good practices, many of them
reflect what was said previously about job advertisements

Quality Attributes
▶Bass et al. (2021) define a quality attribute as “a measurable
or testable property of a system that is used to indicate how
well the system satisfies the needs of its stakeholders beyond
the basic function of the system”
• Thus, a QA is related to stakeholders via their needs
• Yet, a “need ̸= basic function” of a system
▶Notice also the phrasing “measurable or testable”
• Often easier said than done; how to measure security, say?
▶Note also that QAs are not only about source code; many of
them require understanding a system’s runtime behavior

The Antonyms of Quality Attributes
▶There are many antonyms for quality attributes
▶Fairbanks (2010) alongside many others use a term big ball
of mud for a system that has (a) no clear connectors between
elements; (b) not even a single QA promoted; (c) and no
considerations about any constraints or downsides
• Thus: even when all functional requirements have been met,
a system may be a nightmare in terms of software quality
• Concepts such as technical debt convey a message that
many systems are redesigned and re-implemented but seldom
due to their functionalities as such

Quality Attributes as Non-Functional Requirements (1/4)
▶Another way to look at quality attributes is to frame them as
non-functional requirements for a system
▶However, the distinction between functional and
non-functional requirements is fuzzy, confusing, and debated
• A traditional way to frame functional requirements would be
to link them to utility or value (including concrete business
value in the private sector) of stakeholders (incl. customers)
• Yet, problems still exist; Bass et al. (2021) give an example
of designing a control engine; say, if we are designing an
engine for a sports car whose “function” is to drive fast, can it
really be designed without considering performance (QA) and
measurements (e.g., acceleration and other timings) for it?

Quality Attributes as Non-Functional Requirements (4/4)
▶One way to deal with non-functional requirements (QAs) is to
try to decompose them toward functional requirements
▶A QA saying that “a system should be secure” is worthless
• A security-QA saying that “a system should comply with the
PCI DSS standard” is already much better (but not perfect)
▶A QA saying that “a system should scale” is also worthless
• However, a scalability-QA saying that “a web application
should be able to scale up to ten million users after about a
year in operation” is already much better (but not perfect)
▶Yet, overlapping concepts still cause problems, including
with respect to domain knowledge causing tensions too

Quality Attributes via Risks (1/3)
▶Some QAs (such as cyber security) and application domains
(such as safety-critical systems) are often perceived and
modeled through risks and risk analysis
▶The later lectures cover risk-based approaches in more detail;
for the present purposes, it suffices to understand that in
these domains a risk is generally defined as a probability of an
event occurring and its impact upon some assets that are
valuable to stakeholders (including software developers)
• Risk = Probability × Impact; or sometimes also as
• Risk = Probability × Severity × Impact; and so forth

Quality Attributes via Risks (3/3)
▶The risk-based approaches are useful also for prioritizing QAs,
including responses and response measures taken (if any)
▶For instance, consider that Event := “Our small on-premise

means, Fairbanks (2010, p. 150) suggest simple rankings,
such as “scalability > modifiability > security > usability”

Cascading Risks and Impacts (1/4)
▶Regarding fault-tolerance and cyber security, the concepts of
cascading risks and cascading impacts are important
• In other words, there is not just a risk but multiple risks and
their impacts, which are (or may be) related to each other
• Conditional probability is required
▶While these make formal modeling challenging, it is still often
useful to consider them for better understanding risk scenarios
• Say, what happens if a major power failure crashes many
servers, which, in turn, causes traffic to be routed to already
overloaded backup servers, which, in turn, causes congestion
control and throttling to set in?

Software Engineering and Risks More Generally (2/5)
▶Fairbanks (2010, p. 48) argues that software architecture
“efforts should be commensurate with the risk of failure”
• Here, failure is most of all project failure too
▶He continues to elaborate that with a risk analysis one should
find the “hot spots” (with respect to probabilities and
impacts) that must be designed and implemented
▶If you identify a risk needing attention, you should also seek
means to reduce the risk, and preferably with known tactics
and patterns instead of rolling your own ad hoc solutions

Software Engineering and Risks More Generally (4/5)
▶Overwhelmingly, the literature, whether from academia or
industry, considers software engineering and software
architecture risks through negative effects; an impact and its
severity are taken as negative upon some assets
▶However, this is a simplified understanding of risks
• A risk can have positive effects too!
▶Consider the following: “our small startup with a funding for a
project covering two years ends up being a unicorn”
• Regarding design, you would now probably think somewhat
differently upon what to prioritize, including regarding QAs
• For instance: maybe you only really need to scale to 100+
million users once you are a unicorn (and then you can rewrite
the system outputted by your initial small project anyways)?

Software Architecture and Requirements Engineering (2/7)
▶A general problem is that requirement documents – no matter
how rigorously elicited from stakeholders or otherwise written
down, seldom contain explicit non-functional requirements
▶Therefore, QAs must be typically derived or “sniffed” from
the documents, to use the parlance of Bass et al. (2021)
▶In fact, because most requirement documents catalog
functional requirements, most of them do not even concern or
affect an architecture being designed
▶What was said earlier about prioritization is relevant because
a perfect architecture would fulfill all QAs, and, therefore Bass
et al. (2021) use a concept of architecturally significant
requirements (ASRs)

Software Architecture and Requirements Engineering (3/7)
▶For sniffing ASRs from functional requirements and their
documents, there numerous things to look for, including:
1. The perhaps easiest signal would be any explicitly named
technologies or named commercial vendors
2. How about other external elements; is integration required to
other systems, whether software or hardware?
3. Is anything said about deployment; will it run in a cloud, say?
4. What do they say about data; how much, how fast, how often?
5. What can you deduce about networking; will it rely on the
standard TCP/IP stack; are WLAN/5G/Bluetooth/etc.
involved; are there any custom network protocols?
6. Does the intended usage say something about how many users
there will be or whether internationalization is required?
7. What does the domain say about security and legal
requirements; do you need to comply with the GDPR, say?

Quality Gates for ASRs/QAs (1/3)
▶Provided that the ASRs/QAs elicited and defined are indeed
measurable, testable, or even falsifiable, it may be possible to
use quality gates (QGs) already during development
▶The basic idea behind QGs is to provide some checks and
balances especially for project owners or alike; they do not
mean that a development should be halted upon failing to
pass a gate (unless there is a catastrophic situation), but they
rather provide tracking and signals about whether things are
progressing as planned
▶They are mostly applicable for large projects

Reference Architectures and Requirements (1/4)
▶As the name indicates, a software reference architecture
provides a reference, a “guardrail architecture” or a “blueprint
architecture” to follow in “actual” architectures
▶They are useful for promoting reuse of best practices, for
facilitating interoperability, and for conveying the intended
meaning in standards and regulations, among other things
• Thus, they are useful also for understanding the
requirements in a particular domain or of a technology
▶For instance, CPython is the reference architecture and
implementation for an interpreter of the Python programming
language, although it is also the de facto implementation
because solid alternatives have not emerged thus far

Standards and Requirements (1/3)
▶Implicitly or explicitly, standards, including for protocols, are
often dealing with reference architectures at least in theory
▶If working with standardization in itself, it is necessary to

▶Although reference architectures evolve, those bound to

and usually backwards compatibility too
• If an ASR is explicitly embedded to a standard, it may have
far-reaching consequences for decades (or more) to come!

Standards and Requirements (2/3)
▶IETF (1996) has noted the general architectural design issues
affecting the Internet:
1. “Heterogeneity is inevitable and must be supported”
• Heterogeneity is about the compatibility QA category
2. “If there are several ways of doing the same thing,
choose one” (thus, even if you do twin peaks modeling, one
architecture must eventually be chosen for an implementation)
3. “All designs must scale readily to very many nodes per site
and to many millions of sites” (thus, scalability is a QA too)
4. “Performance and cost must be considered as well as
functionality” (thus, two general QAs & one constraint)
5. “Keep it simple. When in doubt during design, choose the
simplest solution” (thus, avoiding complexity is another QA)

▶Yet, a lot of software is designed and developed without any
explicit or particular “stakeholders” – consumer markets,
commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) software, and so forth
▶Modeling helps at defining ASRs/QAs in these many cases
▶By modeling, we do not mean model-driven engineering
strictly speaking; hence, recall what was said in the first

▶By modeling, in the present context, we most of all mean
