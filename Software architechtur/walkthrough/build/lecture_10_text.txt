run a device or a larger system

Total poweri = computing poweri + idle poweri

Preloading (2/2)
▶Preloading can save power because startup is typically
resource-intensive in many contexts
• As it helps with responsiveness too, it can be seen as a
way to deal with the earlier trade-off too (Hort et al. 2022)
▶Analogously, it may sometimes be reasonable to never fully
terminate lightweight processes even in case a user requests so
▶What is being preloaded differs; on a mobile device it could be
applications, while in cloud computing it might be data
▶Predictive analytics can be used to determine what should
be preloaded; e.g., on a mobile device, only a user’s frequently
used applications (based on historical data) could be preloaded

===== PAGE 60 =====
Optimizations More Generally (1/2)
▶From the previous exposition, we can deduce a general rule of
thumb for developers seeking to optimize power consumption:
try to minimize software’s actions that interfere with
optimizations already done for hardware; hence, e.g.,
• Do not cause unnecessary interrupts that lead to hardware
wake-ups (cf. notifications, polling, etc.)
• Do not unnecessarily utilize a disk and a network
• Use preloading, prefetching, and offloading when reasonable
• Try to avoid garbage collector runs (Hort et al. 2022)
• Note that memory is hardware too, and, therefore, many
classical bug types (such as memory leaks) are often also
power optimization bugs (ibid.)

• On the other hand: sometimes you might consider a

Evaluating Architectures and Designs (1/6)
▶Architecture evaluation is a “process of determining the
degree to which an architecture is fit for the purpose for which
it is intended” (Bass et al 2021)
▶Architecture evaluation is also a risk reduction action
• Whether it is about cyber security risks or business risks,
risks must be identified, and after they have been identified,
addressing the risks is a cost/benefit action (ibid.)
• Note that the noun “cost” should be understood loosely;
thus, recall the notion of acceptable risks, which also implies
that there are sometimes unacceptable risks

===== PAGE 65 =====
Evaluating Architectures and Designs (2/6)
▶Bass et al. (2021) recommend:
1. A group of reviewers “individually ensure that they
understand the current state of the architecture”
2. The reviewer group determines beforehand evaluation criteria
(incl. metrics if possible) to guide the review
3. “For each scenario, each reviewer should determine whether
the scenario is satisfied”
4. The reviewers seek to capture potential problems and identify
risks exposed during the prior scenario-based step
▶Although a plural form is used above, a single architect may
also conduct reviews, and further note that outsiders may be
involved in a peer review by multiple professionals

▶Falessi et al. (2010) list numerous factors – among these are:

===== PAGE 68 =====
Evaluating Architectures and Designs (5/6)
▶Rams (2023), a famous industrial designer, formulated ten
principles for good industrial design, some of which resonate
well also with software design (and some perhaps not):
1. Innovative: a design always aligns with technology, and can
thus never be an end in itself
2. Usefulness: a good design is useful, but not only in terms of
functionality, but also in terms of aesthetic quality and
psychological aspects
3. A good design is aesthetic
4. Understandability: a good design clarifies a product’s goals,
structure, and functionality
5. Honesty: a good design does not manipulate users
6. A good design is thorough to the last detail
7. A good design is environmentally friendly
8. A good design involves as little design as possible

===== PAGE 69 =====
Evaluating Architectures and Designs (6/6)
▶The output from an evaluation should be documented
▶Bass et al. (2021) recommend to focus on:
1. A concise overall presentation of the architecture
2. Articulation of business or other goals
3. Prioritization of quality attributes based on scenarios
4. A set of risks, including acceptable risks and non-risks
5. Mapping of architectural decisions to quality attributes
6. Articulation of trade-offs and decisions thereto
▶For reusability and rigor, it may be sometimes a good idea to
document in tabular form (e.g., having architectural decisions
as rows and risks, trade-offs, and impacts as columns)
